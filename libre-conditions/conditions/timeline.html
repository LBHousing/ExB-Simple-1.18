How We Got Here

1930s–1940s · Redlining
The Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) graded Long Beach neighborhoods by perceived "lending risk" — but race was the dominant factor. The map above shows the results: communities of color were coded "Hazardous" (red), cutting them off from mortgage lending and trapping residents in deteriorating housing.
  • Banks refused to lend in red-graded areas — no mortgages, no home equity, no wealth building
  • The FHA adopted similar maps, amplifying the damage across federal housing programs
  • HOLC appraisers explicitly cited the presence of Black, Latino, and Asian residents as risk factors
  • Long Beach's West Side, Central, and North Long Beach were among the hardest hit
1940s–1960s · Racial Covenants
Property deeds across Long Beach included legal restrictions preventing sale to Black, Latino, and Asian residents. Even after the Supreme Court struck down enforcement in 1948, the covenants remained on deeds and the segregation patterns they created were already locked in.
  • Covenants restricted where people of color could buy, reinforcing redlined boundaries
  • White neighborhoods received FHA-backed mortgages; redlined neighborhoods did not
  • The wealth gap from denied homeownership compounds across generations
1960s–1990s · The Disinvestment Cycle
Without access to credit, homeownership, or public investment, redlined neighborhoods entered decades of decline. Property values dropped, maintenance was deferred, and housing stock aged without reinvestment. Landlords extracted rent while investing as little as possible.
  • Fair Housing Act of 1968 outlawed redlining — but the damage was already done
  • Neighborhoods remained segregated by income and race through inertia and ongoing discrimination
  • Aging buildings meant rising code violations, health hazards, and unsafe living conditions
2000s–Present · The Crisis Today
These same neighborhoods now have the highest rates of code violations, the most eviction filings, and the greatest concentration of cost-burdened renters. Corporate entities are buying up properties in these areas, and the tenants — disproportionately BIPOC — face displacement.
  • 53.1% of Long Beach renters are cost burdened — spending 30%+ of income on housing
  • 24.9% of all dwelling units are now owned by LLCs, corporations, and partnerships
  • The Block the Cuts and Build Long Beach Ordinance aims to reinvest in these communities
  • 90 years later, the map of housing distress matches the 1939 redlining map
The pattern is not a coincidence. When you overlay today's code violation data, eviction heat maps, and demographic data, the geography matches the HOLC redlining map above. The disinvestment was deliberate. The consequences are ongoing. The data on this page connects the dots — from the red lines drawn in the 1930s to the eviction notices served today.
